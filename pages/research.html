Our Science

Research at SFG@IISERT

We study how stars and planets are born — from the collapse of molecular clouds to the evolution of protoplanetary disks, the demographics of brown dwarfs, and the physical processes that set the masses of stars across cosmic time.

Research Themes

Evolution of Protoplanetary Disks

Young stars form inside rotating disks of gas and dust. These disks stick around for about 10 million years before they disappear. The material either falls into the star, gets blown away by radiation, or clumps together to form planets.

Brown Dwarfs

Brown dwarfs sit awkwardly between giant planets and small stars. They gather enough mass to spark some early fusion, but not enough to keep it going. Instead of shining like a main-sequence star, they just slowly fade and cool down over billions of years.

Learn more

Role of Metallicity

Gas clouds need heavy elements to cool down efficiently. Without them, the gas stays hot and resists gravity, meaning it takes a much larger clump of material to finally trigger a collapse. Because of this, the metal-poor early universe likely churned out a lot more massive stars than we see today.

Learn more

Initial Mass Function of Stars

The Initial Mass Function (IMF) describes the distribution of star sizes in a new cluster. A star's starting mass determines exactly how it will live and die. Local gas density, available metals, and nearby radiation all affect what sizes of stars a cloud actually ends up producing.

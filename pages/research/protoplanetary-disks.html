Evolution of Protoplanetary Disks

Understanding the birth and dispersal of planet-forming disks around young stars.

Overview

What Are Protoplanetary Disks?

Protoplanetary disks are the dense, rotating circumstellar halos of gas and dust that surround newly formed young stellar objects (YSOs). Serving as the fundamental cradles for planet formation, understanding the evolution and eventual dissipation of these disks is a crucial step in piecing together the origins of planetary systems, including our own solar system.

At the Star and Planet Formation Group (SFG@IISERT), our research investigates the critical factors that dictate how long these disks survive and the mechanisms driving their dispersal. We explore the complex interplay between disk dissipation timescales, the mass of the central star, and the overarching influence of the surrounding galactic environment.

By probing a diverse range of environments - from the immediate solar vicinity to the low-metallicity extremes of the outer Milky Way - we aim to answer pivotal questions about disk longevity. Our group bridges multi-wavelength observational data to construct a comprehensive, empirical picture of protoplanetary disk evolution.

Key Questions

  • How do disk survival timescales vary across different stellar and substellar mass regimes?
  • Why do some discs exist beyond 10 Myr? What is the origin scenario?
  • Does the galactic environment, particularly low metallicity, alter the initial disk fraction and evolutionary pathways?
  • How do the disk properties around brown dwarfs compare to those of typical solar-mass stars?

Methodology

Our Approach

A robust study of disk evolution begins with the accurate identification of young stellar objects and the confirmation of their cluster membership. Whenever possible, we extensively utilize high-precision astrometric and kinematic data from the Gaia mission to refine the census of star-forming regions. In regions or observational regimes where Gaia data is insufficient, we employ rigorous statistical decontamination techniques to ensure our stellar samples remain accurate and free from field star contamination.

To detect the presence and evolutionary stage of protoplanetary disks, we rely on a comprehensive combination of multi-wavelength photometric and spectroscopic data. By leveraging large-scale surveys such as UKIDSS, 2MASS, Spitzer, and WISE, we construct and analyze the Spectral Energy Distributions (SEDs) of these young stars, where an infrared excess serves as our primary observational tracer for warm circumstellar dust. Complementing this, we utilize spectroscopic data to directly probe and verify the active accretion status of these sources.

Beyond identifying disks, we rigorously derive the physical parameters of the central stars. By mapping our observational data against theoretical pre-main-sequence evolutionary models, we determine precise stellar ages and masses. This enables us to calculate accurate disk fractions across distinct age bins and stellar mass regimes, ultimately allowing us to map out the e-folding timescales of disk survival.

Findings

Recent Results

01

Mapping Disk Survival Over 100 Myr

By conducting a comprehensive, Gaia-driven survey of 32 nearby young clusters, we tracked the final stages of disk dissipation. Our results reveal that disk survival is strongly tied to the host star's mass, with distinct e-folding timescales across different wavelengths, dictating exactly how long these planet-forming environments can persist.

02

The Crucial Role of Metallicity

Probing clusters located 10-13 kpc away in the outer Milky Way, we investigated how galactic environment influences disk evolution. We discovered that low-metallicity environments harbor significantly fewer disks - averaging 2.6 times lower than solar-metallicity regions - demonstrating a robust positive correlation between metallicity and disk longevity.

03

Evolution Down to the Substellar Limit

Using a detailed census of the λ Orionis star-forming region, we pushed our evolutionary studies beyond typical stars and down into the brown dwarf regime. We found a clear dependency of disk properties on the central object's mass, shedding light on how planetary cradles evolve around the lowest-mass objects in the galaxy.

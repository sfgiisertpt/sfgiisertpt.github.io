Variability in Young Stellar Objects

Exploring the dynamic nature, rotation, and episodic accretion of pre-main sequence stars through time-domain observations.

Overview

The Dynamic Nature of YSOs

Young Stellar Objects (YSOs) are inherently dynamic, exhibiting significant photometric and spectroscopic variability across multiple timescales. This variability acts as a unique observational window into the complex physical processes governing early stellar evolution, including stellar rotation, magnetic activity, and the dynamic exchange of mass between the central star and its circumstellar disk.

At the Star and Planet Formation Group (SFG@IISERT), we study these time-variable phenomena to reconstruct the accretion histories and angular momentum evolution of young stars. We investigate extreme episodic accretion events—such as FU Orionis outbursts—which can dramatically alter the physical structure of both the star and its surrounding planet-forming environment.

Furthermore, by monitoring the rotational modulation of young stars across different age regimes, we probe the interaction between stellar magnetic fields and their disks. Our research spans from deeply embedded, low-luminosity protostars undergoing sudden growth spurts to older pre-main-sequence stars, tracing how long active star-disk interactions persist.

Key Questions

  • What drives extreme episodic accretion events (e.g., FUor outbursts), especially in very low-luminosity protostars?
  • How does the presence of a circumstellar disk influence stellar rotation rates (disk-locking)?
  • What mechanisms cause photometric variability (dippers, bursters) in older disk-bearing stars?
  • How do star-disk interactions and accretion geometries evolve as the YSO ages?

Methodology

Observing Time-Domain Phenomena

Our study of YSO variability relies heavily on extensive time-domain photometry, utilizing data from high-cadence space missions (like TESS), long-term ground-based optical monitoring surveys (such as ASAS-SN and ZTF), and crucial mid-infrared time-domain data from the NEOWISE mission. By constructing and analyzing these multi-wavelength light curves, we can detect periodic signals caused by cold magnetic spots or hot accretion spots rotating in and out of view.

To analyze periodic variability, we employ robust periodogram techniques (e.g., Lomb-Scargle) to determine stellar rotation periods. For aperiodic and irregular variability, we classify the morphological types of the light curves. We identify "dippers" caused by transient obscuration from inner disk warps and dust clumps, and "bursters" triggered by sudden increases in the accretion rate.

For targets exhibiting extreme, long-term outbursts—such as FU Ori-type events—we complement our photometric monitoring with detailed spectroscopic and high-resolution imaging follow-ups. Utilizing facilities like the NASA Infrared Telescope Facility (IRTF) and the Gemini Observatory, we obtain near-infrared spectra and adaptive optics imaging. This approach allows us to accurately characterize the physical parameters of the episodic accretion event, analyze the outbursting source's distinct spectral features, and probe its immediate circumstellar environment.

Findings

Recent Results

01

A Surprisingly Faint FU Ori-Type Outburst

By tracking a decade of NEOWISE mid-infrared brightening and utilizing Gemini near-IR spectroscopy, we characterized an outburst in the embedded binary SSTgbsJ214706. Strikingly, the outburst reached only ~0.95 solar luminosities—one to two orders of magnitude fainter than standard FU Orionis eruptions. This discovery challenges current accretion paradigms and raises new questions about the mechanisms driving exceptionally low-luminosity protostellar eruptions.

02

Rotation and Disk-Locking in T Tauri Stars

Through comprehensive optical photometric monitoring, we analyzed the periodic variability and rotation rates of T Tauri stars. By comparing rotation periods and variability amplitudes against the presence of circumstellar disks, we observed clear trends of stars spinning down over the T Tauri phase. This provides critical observational support for the disk-locking mechanism, where the magnetic star-disk connection regulates the star's angular momentum.

03

Persistent Accretion in Old Disks

While typical protoplanetary disks dissipate within a few million years, we investigated the variability and accretion signatures of anomalously old disk-bearing stars. We found that while their mass accretion rates are systematically lower than those of younger analogs of similar mass, they follow the same evolutionary trends—demonstrating that active, dynamic star-disk interactions can persist far longer than standard evolutionary models predict.
